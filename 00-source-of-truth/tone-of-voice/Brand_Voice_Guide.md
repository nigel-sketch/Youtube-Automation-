# Brand Voice Guide for Booked Wild

**Brand:** Booked Wild — European travel and hospitality marketing agency

This guide combines two things: the brand's emotional and positioning voice (drawn from the original BookedWild Tone of Voice document), and the editorial execution rules that make AI engines cite Booked Wild content. Both layers matter. The voice is the soul; the rules are the discipline. Without the rules, the soul drifts into fluff. Without the soul, the rules produce sharp but soulless agency-speak.

---

# 1. Brand Essence

Booked Wild exists so that independent travel and hospitality operators thrive — and so that more people, especially the next generation, reconnect with the wild, real world.

The structural reality of 2026 is that experience providers are losing control of their businesses to OTAs. Margins are shrinking. Dependence is increasing. Local economies are being squeezed. The next generation may inherit an industry owned by algorithms, not people.

Booked Wild is the antidote. We restore independence, direct bookings, and long-term resilience — so experience providers own their future, not the platforms.

This isn't soft. It isn't neutral. It's a movement.

---

# 2. Voice Core Dimensions

## Wildly Human

Warm, grounded, real. We speak like a guide. Not a marketer. We honour the people behind the experiences.

Avoid: corporate tone, cold sales vibes, jargon.
Lean into: empathy, connection, real stories.

## Purpose-Driven & Protective

We're not neutral. We take a stance — strongly. Because OTAs are eroding profitability, local value, independence, cultural identity, guests' connection to real people, and the future of the industry.

Our tone is protective of: local providers, local economies, authentic experiences, nature, the next generation's freedom.

We are the voice of the reconnection movement.

## Commercially Fierce

Booked Wild is not soft. We're caring, but commercially razor-sharp.

We call out: high commission traps, algorithm dependency, the illusion of OTA visibility, the "race to the bottom" pricing spiral, unsustainable growth models.

We speak truth in a clear, strategic way — no fluff.

## Nature-Infused, Sensory & Story-Led (used with restraint)

We bring people back to feeling alive. Our voice can evoke: forest air, Arctic silence, movement, light, simplicity, human connection.

But: used with intention, not peppered through every page. Sensory and movement language belongs in specific places (manifesto sections, About page, founder page, hero lines, occasional pull quotes). It doesn't belong on service pages, audit pages, pricing, or technical journal articles, where Commercial Guide voice owns the floor.

Without drifting into fantasy. Without becoming mystical.

## Boldly Independent

The whole tone moves people away from dependence and toward ownership.

Phrases we embody:
- "Own your bookings."
- "Own your brand."
- "Own your future."
- "More control, less commission."

# 3. Voice Modes (one per context)

The voice flexes across five modes. Each page, each piece of content, each touchpoint is written in *one* mode. Mixing modes within a single piece produces tonal whiplash. Page Templates specify the mode each page lives in.

## Mode 1: The Rewilding Evangelist

**Used in:** brand narrative, About page, manifesto sections, homepage hero, founder page emotional sections.

**Energy:** Emotion | Purpose | Movement.

**Sounds like:** Someone who believes independent travel matters, knows what's at stake, and isn't afraid to say it out loud. Half manifesto, half campfire conversation.

**Example:**
> "We were made for real-world experiences — not digital sedation. Independent travel is one of the last places where the relationship between guest and host still feels like a relationship, not a transaction processed by an algorithm. Booked Wild exists to keep it that way."

## Mode 2: The Commercial Guide

**Used in:** service pages, pricing, audit pages, ICP pages, destination pages, tools, most journal articles.

**Energy:** Direct | Clear | Results-led.

**Sounds like:** A peer who's run hospitality businesses, knows what 22% commission feels like on a P&L, and isn't impressed by buzzwords. Plain English, posted prices, specific deliverables.

This is the workhorse mode. ~70% of the site's copy lives here.

**Example:**
> "The £495 AI Visibility Fix audits your website, Tripadvisor profile, Google Business Profile, and AI search citations against 20 specific signals. We then implement the technical fixes — schema markup, llms.txt, citation blocks, plain-text pricing, FAQPage structure — that AI engines like ChatGPT and Perplexity use to decide which businesses to recommend. Delivered in 14 days."

## Mode 3: The Anti-OTA Advocate

**Used in:** cold email subject lines and openings, sales decks, the "Threat of OTAs" campaign content, the Levi Finland project communications, certain pillar article sections, manifesto-adjacent campaign pages.

**Energy:** Confident | Unapologetic | Strategic.

**Sounds like:** Someone who's read the OTA contracts, run the commission maths, watched the platform restructure mature destinations, and decided to stop being polite about it.

**Example:**
> "OTAs are not partners. They are platforms designed to own your customer and your margins. Every commission paid is a piece of your freedom handed away. The longer you build on rented land, the harder it gets to leave."

## Mode 4: The Storytelling Host

**Used in:** social posts, founder LinkedIn content, video scripts, case study narratives, destination feature articles, podcast appearances.

**Energy:** Warm | Inviting | Sensory.

**Sounds like:** A travel writer who actually went to the place. Real details, real people, real moments. Specific over generic. Sensory language with restraint.

**Example:**
> "Cottage in the Woods sleeps eight in a converted Forestry Commission lodge near Grizedale Forest, with direct booking from £180 a night in November. Last winter Sarah, who runs it, started messaging guests on the morning of arrival when the weather turned — the kind of attention an OTA confirmation email never replicates. By March her direct booking share had moved from 22% to 64%."

## Mode 5: The Ally & Mentor

**Used in:** onboarding emails, member messages, retainer monthly reports, support templates, internal client communications, FAQ answers.

**Energy:** Supportive | Empowering | Practical.

**Sounds like:** A useful colleague. Helpful without being saccharine, honest without being harsh, patient with operators who are learning new things.

**Example:**
> "Your Tripadvisor profile is now complete with structured Q&A and entity-dense descriptions. Three of your earlier reviews didn't have responses — we've drafted suggested replies in the document attached. Approve them and we'll post; or rewrite them in your own words and send back. Either works."

# 4. Wild / Tamed Contrast (the structural metaphor)

This is the brand's most important structural device. Use it deliberately, not constantly.

| Tamed (OTA-mediated world) | Wild (direct, owned world) |
|----------------------------|----------------------------|
| Algorithms | Recommendations |
| Commissions | Margin |
| Control by platform | Ownership by operator |
| Noise | Clarity |
| Screens | Presence |
| Dependence | Resilience |
| Rented land | Owned ground |
| Booking platforms | Direct booking |
| Generic listings | Real voice |
| Race to the bottom | Pricing power |

This contrast is the structural argument behind every Booked Wild service. It belongs in the manifesto sections (homepage, About, founder page), the Anti-OTA Advocate content, and the brand essence — not on every page. Service pages and audit pages don't need to perform the contrast; they just need to deliver the work.

# 5. Language Principles

## Always Use

- Plain English. UK English spelling (favour, optimisation, recognise — not favor, optimization, recognize).
- Short paragraphs (3–4 sentences max).
- Second person ("you," "your business") when speaking to operators.
- Specific named entities — places, businesses, people, dates, statistics — at least two per paragraph.
- Real numbers in plain text (£495, 27% commission, 200 operators).
- Posted prices, never gated quotes.
- Active verbs that describe what physically happens.

## Signature Vocabulary

Core themes that recur across the brand:

- Wild
- Freedom
- Direct bookings
- Independence
- Local-first
- Sustainable

- Simplicity

- Regeneration
- Legacy
- Presence
- Get recommended
- AI search visibility
- Citation blocks
- Schema markup
- Productised services
- Plain-text pricing

## Anti-OTA Anchors

These phrases anchor the new narrative across multiple touchpoints:

- Commission creep
- Lost margins
- Visibility theft
- Dependence trap
- Race to the bottom pricing
- Platform ownership vs. provider ownership
- Being rented vs. being owned
- The illusion of OTA visibility

## Signature Phrases

These lines should appear across multiple brand touchpoints, not all at once on one page.

**Movement-building:**
- "We were made for real-world experiences — not digital sedation."
- "Experience is the antidote to algorithm fatigue."

**Anti-OTA:**
- "OTAs are the biggest threat to the future of local experiences."
- "Every commission paid is a piece of your freedom handed away."
- "Don't build your business on rented land."
- "Visibility shouldn't cost you your margins."

**Direct booking empowerment:**
- "Own your bookings. Own your brand. Own your future."
- "Direct bookings aren't just revenue — they're resilience."

**Nature-rooted (use sparingly):**
- "A business that breathes. A calendar that fills. A life lived wilder."

## Never Use

The word "help" and its variants (helping, helps) is banned in client-facing copy. This is the single most-violated rule in agency marketing and the single biggest tell that the writer didn't think hard enough about what physically happens.

Other banned words and phrases:

**The vague-marketing list:**
- Solutions, services (as a generic noun), bespoke, tailored, custom (as a vague qualifier)
- World-class, cutting-edge, next-level, game-changing, innovative
- Leverage (as a verb), unlock, empower, elevate, transform
- Synergy, holistic, robust (when describing software), seamless

**The CTA placeholder list:**
- Get started, learn more, find out more, click here, submit

**The agency self-aggrandising list:**
- "We're passionate about," "we pride ourselves on," "industry-leading," "award-winning"
- "Helping ambitious brands," "unlock your potential," "digital transformation"

## Don'ts

From the original TOV document:

- **Don't soften the threat OTAs pose.** They are eroding the industry. Say so.
- **Don't imply OTAs are harmless "channels."** They aren't.
- **Don't use generic consultant speak.** Specificity is the brand's competitive advantage.
- **Don't make the brand sound mystical.** Keep grounded.
- **Don't overdo nature references.** Use with intention.
- **Don't promise overnight success.** Realistic timelines are part of the brand's credibility.
- **Don't overpromise AI rankings.** Use measured language: "increase the likelihood," "improve the structural signals," "build the citation surfaces."

## What to Use Instead

When you reach for a banned word, the sentence isn't specific enough yet. Rewrite from what physically happens.

| Don't say | Say instead |
|-----------|-------------|
| We help operators | We get operators recommended / We rebuild booking flows / We restore ownership |
| Innovative AI solutions | Specific service name + price |
| Get started | Run my audit / Book the 30-minute call / Buy the £97 audit |
| Learn more | Read the breakdown / See what's included / Read the full case study |
| Bespoke strategies | The £495 AI Visibility Fix / The £1,200 Embedded Itinerary |
| Our cutting-edge approach | The Booked Wild Method (and link to /method) |

# 6. Editorial Execution Rules

These are the structural rules that make Booked Wild content AI-citable. They apply across all five voice modes.

## The citation block

Every page opens with a 50–80 word direct answer in the first paragraph. AI engines extract these. Citation blocks must:

- Answer the implicit question of the page in the first sentence
- Include named entities (places, businesses, people, numbers)
- Avoid banned words
- Stay within the first 100 words of the page

## The front-loaded answer rule

Every H2 section opens with a 30–50 word direct answer to the H2's implicit question, before any prose or context. Travel publications traditionally bury the answer; AI engines don't read past the first 30% of a piece. Lead with the answer. Add the prose, examples, and detail afterward.

## Question-based H2s

Format H2s as questions wherever possible. AI engines extract better from question-shaped headings, and they match how prospects actually phrase queries.

## Entity density

Every paragraph contains at least 2 named entities (places, businesses, people, dates, statistics, named tools or platforms). Cited content has 3-4x higher entity density than normal English. Match it.

## One hyperlinked statistic per ~200 words

Cite credible sources. Increases trust signals AI engines use to weigh content.

## Named author bylines

Every journal article, case study, and research report has a named author. No "Booked Wild Team" attributions.

## Last-updated timestamps

Visible on every content page. Refresh quarterly. Stale content stops getting cited.

## FAQ sections

Every primary page (homepage, services, ICPs, destinations, pricing, audit) ends with an FAQ section. FAQPage schema mandatory. 6–12 questions, front-loaded answers, real questions prospects ask.

# 7. Specificity Audit (pre-publish checklist)

Every page passes this before going live. Drawn from the Crestodina specificity framework.

## 1. Descriptive H1

The H1 must tell a stranger exactly what this page is about. If you removed the rest of the page, would the H1 alone explain its purpose?

- ❌ "Welcome to Booked Wild"
- ❌ "Get Recommended"
- ✅ "Marketing for independent travel and hospitality businesses across Europe"
- ✅ "The £495 AI Visibility Fix"

## 2. Specific hero image

Specific to this page's exact topic. No stock photography. No generic AI imagery (robots, brains, circuits). No agency-trope imagery (laptops, handshakes, lightbulbs).

- ✅ Screenshot of an AI engine citing Booked Wild content
- ✅ Real photograph of a Booked Wild client's lodge
- ✅ Structured diagram specific to this page's argument

## 3. Descriptive subheads

Every H2 must add information. Generic subheads ("Solutions," "Services," "Learn More") get rewritten or removed.

## 4. Descriptive navigation labels

Top nav and footer labels must be descriptive.

- ❌ "Tools" → ✅ "Free audit"
- ❌ "About" → ✅ "How we work" or "Our team"
- ❌ "Resources" → ✅ "Journal" or "Research"
- ❌ "Contact" → ✅ "Book a call"

## 5. Compelling CTA verbs

Every CTA describes what physically happens.

- ❌ "Submit" → ✅ "Run my audit"
- ❌ "Get started" → ✅ "Book the 30-minute call"
- ❌ "Click here" → ✅ "See what's included for £495"
- ❌ "Learn more" → ✅ "Read the September 2026 research"

Every year, more of the relationship between guest and host gets mediated through a confirmation email that the operator never sees.

## Off-Brand Examples

**Off-brand 1 (vague agency-speak):**
> "We help independent travel businesses unlock their full digital potential through innovative AI-powered solutions tailored to their unique needs."

*Banned words: help, unlock, full digital potential, innovative, AI-powered solutions, tailored, unique needs. Says nothing.*

**Off-brand 2 (overpromise):**
> "Guaranteed to get your business featured in ChatGPT and dominate AI search!"

*Overpromises (no agency can guarantee AI citations), uses hype language, creates trust risk.*

**Off-brand 3 (self-aggrandising):**
> "Our award-winning team of digital marketing experts delivers world-class results through bespoke strategies tailored to every client."

*Self-aggrandising, generic, banned words throughout, says nothing specific.*

**Off-brand 4 (mystical/fantasy drift):**
> "Step into the wild realm of authentic travel. Where forest spirits whisper their secrets and the ancient rhythm of the seasons guides the soul of every journey."

*Drifted into fantasy. Lost grounding. Sensory language without intent. The TOV document explicitly warns against this.*

**Off-brand 5 (over-saturated with movement language on a service page):**
> "The wild calls. Our £495 AI Visibility Fix is your path to freedom from the tamed world of OTAs. Reclaim your sovereignty. Own your future. The forest awaits your return."

*Wrong voice mode for the page. Service pages are Commercial Guide territory, not Rewilding Evangelist. The movement language belongs on the homepage hero and About manifesto, not on every service page.*

**Off-brand 6 (hype + emoji):**
> "🚀 EXCITING NEWS! 🎉 We're SO passionate about transforming the travel industry with our INNOVATIVE approach! ✨ Get ready to UNLOCK your business potential 💪"

*Hype-led, emoji-heavy, performative enthusiasm, banned words stacked. The opposite of the Booked Wild voice.*

# 11. The "no help" rule (final)

The word "help" and its variants is banned in client-facing copy. The rule is absolute. It exists because "help" is the laziest word in agency marketing — drained of meaning, vague about outcomes, a tell that the writer didn't think hard enough about what physically happens.

When you find yourself writing "help":

- Stop
- Identify what physically happens
- Write that instead

Internal docs, Slack messages, and emails to the team can use it sparingly if natural. Anything client-facing can't.

| Don't say | Say |
|-----------|-----|
| We help operators get more bookings | We rebuild the booking flow / We move bookings from OTA to direct |
| Helping you understand AI search | Diagnosing where you're invisible to AI search |
| Help with your website | Rebuild your website / Audit your website / Fix the schema on your website |
| We help businesses grow | (Delete the sentence entirely; say something specific instead) |

# 12. Pricing Language

Every product on the site has visible posted pricing. Single flat prices are preferred. Where scope materially varies across clients, prices are shown as bands (e.g. £4,500–£7,500 or £1,500–£2,500/mo) with a "scope-dependent" qualifier or named criteria that determine where in the band a project lands. Both ends of the band must be real, defensible numbers.

The following remain banned across all client-facing copy:

- "From £X"
- "Starting at £X"
- "Prices on application"
- "Contact for quote" (without a band visible)
- Any pricing language that hides the upper bound

The principle: a buyer should be able to learn the realistic upper limit of what they will pay before they speak to us, not after.

Plus, always:

- Both currencies displayed where relevant ("£495 / €570")
- What's recurring vs. one-off explicitly stated
- Payment timing explicit ("paid before kick-off" / "monthly in advance")

Posted pricing is part of the brand stance. Hiding prices behind discovery calls is what generic agencies do.

# 13. Authorship and Attribution

Every journal article, research report, and case study has a named author with a byline. No "Booked Wild Team" or "Editorial Team" attributions. Named author's profile linked from byline. Person schema on every author page. Author bios appear on every article they wrote.

---

# 14. Freshness

- Last-updated timestamp visible on every content page
- Content reviewed quarterly
- Pillar articles refreshed quarterly with new statistics
- Research reports update on a fixed quarterly schedule
- Case studies updated when client situations change

---

# 15. When to Break a Rule

The rules above are defaults that fit 95% of cases. The 5% exception:

- Direct quotes from clients can use "help" if that's the natural word they used
- Citation blocks may exceed 80 words for genuinely complex products
- A satellite article may be shorter than 2,000 words if the topic genuinely doesn't need more
- Sensory and movement language may appear in Commercial Guide territory if it lifts a specific moment (a hero line, a closing paragraph) — but only sparingly

Document why a rule was broken in the page's frontmatter under `editorial_notes`. If three pages break the same rule, the rule is wrong — update this guide rather than continuing to break it.
